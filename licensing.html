1. Where have you published the rights statement for this dataset?

Provide the URL to a page that describes the right to re-use this dataset. This should include a reference to its licence or copyright waiver, attribution requirements, and a statement about relevant copyright. A rights statement helps people understand what they can and can't do with the data.

2. Is there any copyright in this data?

No, the data only contains facts and numbers.

There is no copyright in factual information. If the data does not contain any content that was created through intellectual effort, there are no rights in the content.

Copyright only applies to data if you spent intellectual effort creating what's in it, for example, by writing text that's within the data, or deciding whether particular data is included. There's no copyright if the data only contains facts where no judgements were made about whether to include them or not.

3. Is the data marked as Public Domain?

Works in the public domain are those whose intellectual property rights have expired, have been forfeited, or are inapplicable. If none of the content of your data have copyright or other rights over it, it is good practice to explicitly state this by labelling it as ‘public domain’. You can do this by using the Creative Commons Public Domain Mark or a no known rights statement.

4. Under which licence or waiver can people reuse this data?

Remember that whoever spends intellectual effort creating content automatically gets rights over it. Creative content includes the organisation and selection of items within data, but does not include facts. The CC0 is a document in which the user purports to abandon all claim to copyright and related rights. It is not a licence. Whilst you could apply a CC0 Waiver, there are some doubts about its force under Australian law, particularly with respect to moral rights. Furthermore, the disclaimer that accompanies CC0, at present, may be ineffective in protecting the user from liability for claims of negligence.

11. Where are the rights and licencing of third-party data explained?

Give the URL for a page where you describe how someone can find out the rights and licensing of third-party data nested within the dataset.

In some data, the rights in different records are held by different people or organisations. Where these nested rights carry different restrictions, the dataset will inherit, in total, the most restrictive of the nested restrictions.

For example, if two nested elements of data are licensed CC-BY and CC-BY-NC, the entire dataset will need to be licensed CC-BY-NC, which by definition would prevent it from being ‘open’ data. There may be an exception to this rule if the user can clearly identify the nested data elements and the restrictions that apply to them.
